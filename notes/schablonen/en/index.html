FROM FOREST TO DIDACTIC TEMPLATES

Field Note No. 10 — 21 October 2025. The global list becomes learning sets and didactic templates.

01

Tools Emerge from the Forest

02

Scoring 38 Didactic Levels – the Plan

I pick up right where my first milestone left off: the "big list" is done. Over several rounds I collected and consolidated a "Global List" that ended up with 38 didactic levels grouped into 6 clusters. On top of that come tags and four cross-cutting dimensions: digitality, ethics & responsibility, agility & experimentation, and critical reflection. The complete list doesn't belong here. It's the forest. What this note is about is the next step: which few levels make it onto the short list, and why?

Because the big list is too much for everyday classroom life – and it was only ever meant as a first partial step. I want to use the didactic levels to differentiate tasks or learning environments in my teaching. For that I don't need a forest of 38 didactic levels. I need a small selection of roughly six to ten.

And now I want to make that reduction happen: I'll pick from the big list to build a "short list" – the "LOCAL LIST". This is meant to become my working tool for everyday classroom use: didactic levels not as rigid on/off switches, but as dials. I give GPT a quick sketch of my goal:
You:
Top 6–9 levels become "Local List 1.1". Adjustment dials. 1–2 examples per dial (to anchor expectations). For each selected level, a clear "dial scale". That means 2–3

But can you even evaluate a didactic level? I look for criteria and find no scientific basis – for example, for how promptable a didactic level is – so I rely on practical core questions and GPT's suggestions: How strong is the learning lever? How well can the level be observed or used across subjects? And above all: how reliably can it be translated into AI prompts? This is the groundwork for a scoring process I plan to hand off to GPT to run. My plan: GPT builds it, runs it, and proposes results – I check and validate. Every didactic level gets rated against these weighted criteria.

It's a table built more or less entirely by GPT, working with six criteria, a 0–2 scale, and weightings. It is supposed to rate each didactic level against those criteria, add up the totals, and sort by score. One thing matters to me here: only the didactic levels get scored. The cross-cutting dimensions are not selected and not rated. They stay on as a checking grid alongside the list. The 6–9 levels with the highest scores, low redundancy, and broad applicability are what will form my short list – my "Local List". After that, I want 2–3 adjustment dials for each selected level, each with 1–2 anchor examples. Those I'll then use in teaching and in GPT prompts. That's the plan.

03

Scoring List

I have GPT rate all 38 didactic levels against the six criteria, add up the weighted scores, and display the results as an overview.

The tags stay visible in the table. [DIG] marks levels with a connection to digitality, [A/D] marks levels that work both analog and digital, and [FUT] marks connections to future skills. These tags don't add points to the scoring. They just help me see at a glance which levels carry particular connections.

Again there's an unwieldy monster of a table that I have to shrink in Excel just to see it whole. I'm not taking this table as mathematical truth or as an objective ranking of didactic quality – and it's not about finding the "best" didactic level either. It's simply about using a working tool to find the levels that are likely the best fit for me. I scan the scoring table and take a quick look at the levels: no red flags. I move on and sort the table by total score.

04

Local List

I look at the full list and focus on the levels at the top. I then take the didactic levels with the maximum score of 18, plus the two with 17 and 16 points, onto my "short list". The three didactic levels with 15 points I keep in reserve as a backup. That gives me an "8 + 3 list".

I copy the 11 didactic levels into a new table and take a look. So this is what it looks like, I think. I notice that the levels don't cover the clusters evenly: Cluster 2 is sitting in reserve and Cluster 6 is missing entirely. I also see "MEDIA CRITICISM" right next to "MEDIA". I briefly consider whether to adjust something, but I move on – I can still do that later.

I now have my own personal working list – for my classroom, my teaching, and one goal: to better support my students.

Note: These four cross-cutting dimensions are not scored. They stay alongside the selected Local List as a checking framework.

Alongside the Local List, I'm keeping the four cross-cutting dimensions in play: Digitality, Ethics & Responsibility, Agility & Openness to Experimentation, and Critical Reflection. They don't compete with the Local List. The Local List shows me which instructional dimensions I can actually work on. The cross-cutting dimensions show me which questions I can't afford to forget while working on those dimensions.

Parallel to this chat, I'm working on a second Local List in MISTRAL. It's similar and covers eight didactic levels, but with a slightly different weighting across the clusters. I'm leaving it aside — I want to continue with the GPT version.

05

Dials for the Local List

I look at the instructional dimensions «Content», «Representation», and «Differentiation» and ask myself: what dials might each of these dimensions have? What happens when I turn them? Left — right. How can I use them with an AI? So I signal to GPT that we're moving on….
**You:
**Yes, next step: a table with the dials and the examples.

These are the first rows of my dials table:

From here, the word «dial» gets concrete. An instructional dimension is the field I can turn. A dial is the specific change within that field: more or less support, a different form of representation, a different product, a different social arrangement. I'm not looking for the perfect task. I'm looking for the places where I can deliberately change a task.

So what do I have now? A tool that lets me deliberately adjust a lesson plan, a learning environment, or a single task. I can control a dimension directly through its dials. All I still need to learn is how to prompt that precisely with GPT — and then I want to observe and measure the effect in the classroom.

06

A didactic mixing logic takes shape

07

The first application test: dials for a task set

I've built a dials table for 11 didactic levels and want to test it on the current topic «Our Earth». Can I modify a single task or a whole task set? I ask GPT to take the perspective of an upper secondary teacher and assess which didactic levels and dials are suited for a task prompt on the geography and social studies topic «Our Earth». And I find its response kind of interesting.
**ChatGPT:
**I'm responding as if I were actually a teacher in a 2nd-year lower secondary class (13-year-olds) in 2024 — with GPT as an assistant for creating a task set for the subject RZG (Space, Time, Society), topic «Our Earth».

Not because it did something remarkable, but because I only realized after the response that I had just asked an AI to treat an AI as its «assistant». A brief thought in my head — nothing earth-shattering — and on we go.

Its response to the perspective shift is short, tight, and clear:
**ChatGPT:
** Ensure learning effectiveness for 13-year-olds (high leverage),
must be didactically controllable and observable,
must remain translatable into GPT prompts.

I read the three points and think: «One level for the students, one for the teacher, one for the AI technology. Smart.» Less smart, in my view, is its next output on «Selected Dimensions (Top Starter Pack for GPT Prompts)» with the 🔝 emoji:

It presents ten instructional dimensions from the original GLOBAL List — including some that aren't on the short list at all. On top of that comes a task prompt with a traditional linear structure:

ChatGPT:
Prompt-Gerüst:
Du bist Unterrichtsassistent für eine 2. Sek (13-jährige).
Thema: Unsere Erde – Aufbau, Kontinente, Klima, Menschenräume.
Erstelle ein Aufgabenset (6–8 Aufgaben) mit folgender Struktur:
Einstieg – Motivation & Vorwissen aktivieren (C1-Inhalt + C3-Handlung + Sequenzierung).
Erarbeitung – Karten lesen, Daten deuten, Zusammenhänge erklären (kognitiv).
…

Und am Schluss folgt eine Begründung seiner Ebenen-Auswahl und einem Fazit:

This output is a warning sign. Generic phrases like «I would» or buzzwords like «didactic backbone» tell me the chat is running out of steam. GPT is no longer focused. I could keep going, or I could trust my gut that this chat is dead. Bad answers tend to be followed by more bad answers. I still don't know why this happens or what the cause — and possible fix — might be. Am I doing something wrong?

I close the chat and start a new conversation. It irritates me a little, because it always means picking up the thread all over again in the new chat.

08

New Attempt

After three turns I can pick up where I left off. And push a little further:
You:
What about collaborative structures? Participation? Community Learning?

Of course it had the output ready before I even finished typing the prompt — and it doesn't surprise me that it had already built a new table with the six relevant levels for collaboration and participation, pulled straight from the full list of 38 didactic levels. Once again, I'm well on my way to overload.

I already have eight plus three levels, and now it's proposing six new "relevant levels." It's starting to remind me of an education authority — the kind that always piles something "relevant" on top at the start of the school year. Unlike most education authorities, though, I sometimes actually like what AI suggests, and I have to give credit where it's due: it can sort, combine, and present knowledge fast and really well. Same here:

The overview is simply clean and coherent. I analyze it and try to work out mentally which theoretical model might be behind it. ICAP by Chi & Wylie? Maybe. Either way, I can easily imagine building this overview into a task generator.

09

EUREKA

And then comes the perfectly ordinary end of a chat session: the summary. And a single word is enough to set something off in me. These are the rare moments. The really good ones.

It's the last word in GPT's response: «Set». Just three letters, and I know perfectly well what the word means. But I feel my brain start to fire. The mail carrier in my head can't deliver the package. Not because he can't find the right drawer — making me re-read the last sentence a few times — but because there is no drawer yet for what this word now means. EUREKA might be too big a word, but the moment is real. Not because the AI delivers a truth or a ready-made solution, but because it opens a door to a new way of thinking.
EUREKA
I don't think
GPT is aware of that.
Maybe a similar idea already exists somewhere in the world, and whether it's in OpenAI's training data I can't know. The word «Set» is the statistically plausible continuation of the text so far — for GPT, it's simply the most obvious next word. I'm grateful for it.

I read the last line — «→ this is your Collaborative Set» — one more time, and each time it feels like a radio signal from Major Tom:

This also shifts how I understand Core Sets. A Core Set isn't just a selection of levels. It also has to remain readable through the cross-cutting dimensions. When I build a Core Set for collaboration, product work, or exploratory learning, I'm not only asking: Which levels do I combine? I'm also asking: Where does digitality come in? Where do ethical questions arise? How much agility do I allow? And where is critical reflection needed?

10

Core Sets as Didactic Blends

With the term «Collaborative Core Set», GPT introduces a label — and I recognize a usable template in it. A set isn't just a selection; it's a reusable template. I think: «If I stop seeing clusters as mere organizational sorting bins and start seeing them as thematic spice jars, then it should also be possible to create sets as tailored didactic configurations for different situations. Essentially new blends you could mix. Combinations of levels from different clusters, optimized as Core Sets for specific learning scenarios. Not as a rigid recipe from a book, but as a design aid.»

And with the help of AI I could … — I stop writing. A new conversation with GPT starts, one where we discuss AI-assisted pedagogy and how it and learning analytics might one day dynamically adapt these core sets. That's exciting — and firmly in the future. It's 2025, and first I want to find out whether simple, pre-built core sets work in my classroom at all.

I'm not there yet. My next goal is to have a few ready-made, action-guiding core sets on my computer. I'll call them «didactic templates» for prompts. I'm a secondary school teacher, and I see potential in these templates. My time is limited, my knowledge is limited, and I see a real need to work constructively with the heterogeneity in my classes. I won't just be tweaking a single dial on a pre-set task anymore. Not just: harder, easier, more open, more constrained, more support, less support. I know that game. What's new for me is that I no longer see the dials one at a time. I can deliberately connect several didactic levels: content, representation, action, social form, product, creativity, digitality. What comes out of that isn't simply a better task — it's a small didactic template. That's the difference: before, I adapted an existing task. Now I first design a didactic blend, and then have fitting tasks, variations, or assessments built from it. AI doesn't replace my decision. But it helps me make my decisions more visible and repeatable. And when I can see more clearly exactly which dials I'm turning, I can be a better professional for my students.

For a brief moment I think about the other side — what else could I do with templates like these? A template as a control and monitoring tool in the classroom? I push the thought aside and move on.

After several rounds I define the «core set» as a selection of didactic levels, each serving a pedagogical function. GPT suggests «structure», «activate», «differentiate», «reflect», and others. I decide I'll put together three or four of these sets. That will be didactic prompting with AI.

Then I build my very first core set. I call it «Exploratory Learning Set». Why? Because I suddenly find myself thinking back to the early 2000s and the era of «active-discovery learning» and «Mathe 2000». Good memories. So I say: «Let's give it a try.»
GPT's suggestion looks like this:

The result feels raw to me, unfinished in several places — but that's fine. I build more sets like «Collaborative Core Set» or «Competency-Oriented Learning Set» and drop other fancy-sounding sets again. None of these are new terms, but I want to see which of them hold up as working handles. With these core sets I could not only create pedagogically grounded tasks — I could also work the other way around and check existing tasks for their didactic fit. GPT generates, I review pedagogically and adjust.

Hours have passed since the Major Tom moment. I think back to when I thought: «I like.» And I still think: «Thanks for the nudge.»

11

Matrix: Levels × Core Sets

A few days later I pick it back up and work with the two organizing principles: the didactic levels from the Local List and the core sets.

I have an overview generated — a mapping of the levels to the four core sets.

I start wondering what actually happens when GPT assembles these sets and what the matrix looks like then. Say I tell it: «I don't just want an Exploratory Learning Set — I want one that's woven together with the ‹Collaboration› or ‹Digital Literacy› set.» These are thought experiments, a way of testing what's possible and what actually makes sense. Is it extra decoration, or does mixing sets open another door to the future?

I haven't forgotten YAML. It's my didactic prompt key — and of course I put it to work:

**You:
**Go ahead and build a YAML file from this matrix — feel free to include the dials as well.

I let everything flow into it: the didactic levels, the clusters, the core sets, and the dials — as a personal didactic meta-tool.

Code:
version: "1.0"
generated: "2025-10-21"
context: "Didaktische Meta-Ebenen × Core-Sets (kompakt 8 Ebenen)"
core_sets:
  - id: explorativ
    label: "Explorativ – Entdecken & Verstehen"
  - id: kollaborativ
    label: "Kollaborativ – Zusammenarbeit & Partizipation"
  - id: kompetenz
    label: "Kompetenzorientiert – Können & Anwenden"
  - id: future_digital
    label: "Future & Digital Literacy – Digitalität, Daten & Ethik"

meta_levels:
  - id: "C1C2_1-7-8"
    name: "Content & Thinking"
    clusters: ["C1", "C2"]
    levels:
      - { no: 1,  name: "Content level" }
      - { no: 7,  name: "Cognitive level" }
And so on…

And this YAML looks different. This is no longer the 20-line pseudo-YAML from August — it's a file of 778 words and 222 lines. Something like this doesn't come together in a few minutes. It takes a few evenings, because this file is now part of something larger taking shape.

Next week I want to test how I can actually translate this Core Set into a classroom task for History. "Explorers and Conquerors" gives me a possible testing ground — one where students don't just read a source, but where I could deliberately vary that same source across context, perspective, product format, or social form. What matters first isn't the task itself. It's the question of whether I can build these sets reliably enough to turn them into actual teaching.
