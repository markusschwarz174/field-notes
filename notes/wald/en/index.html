Field Note No. 08 · 2 September 2025

From question to didactic forest

A large collection of didactic levels, clusters and orders grows out of a simple list - and it becomes too large for teaching.

01

The Forest of Didactics

02

How Many Didactic Levels Are There in the World?

It's September 2, 2025, and I've been failing for days at getting GPT to automatically recognize task numbers. To get some distance, I turn to the second part of my project: the didactic analysis of tasks and the question of which levels they can be changed on. My first prompt is a direct assignment. I ask it to differentiate a geography task from Our Earth by level — which it can do, of course. I look at the result and at the same time start thinking about didactic levels. These are the shared language between the tasks in the student textbook and materials like my lesson plan or the starter sheet. I set the task aside and turn to those levels instead.

In my teacher training, I had to memorize the basic didactic levels. I try to list them now — and five still come to mind. Then I give GPT two didactic levels and ask for more:

You:
From a scientific standpoint, we have a content level and an action level. Are there others? Which levels can we modify — in the sense of adaptive learning?

It gives me a list that feels exactly like the didactic levels I had to memorize thirty years ago. Seven levels:

"Haven't seen these in a while" — that's my first thought when I look at the overview. These are the classic levels from my training, and I wouldn't have been able to list them all anymore. Content, action, social dynamics…. "There must be more," I think — and just like that, my curiosity has got me again.

It doesn't just add motivation, assessment, and technology — it adds half a dozen more adjustment screws. I'm not collecting them for the sake of collecting. I have an idea. I want to use didactic levels to differentiate my planning, my teaching, and my tasks more precisely. So I start keeping a running log. After just two rounds, the list has grown.

03

And in the Future?

Since my first chat with GPT, I've been asking about future developments. It's the fascination of the illusion — that you can look ahead. I know it's somewhere between fiction and probability. Still, I want to know which didactic levels might matter in the future. Not the ones I'm about to read and already know. The ones I never would have thought of myself.

You:
Do you have any more levels that matter in terms of future relevance and connectivity?

It names ten didactic levels that could be relevant for the future. Some are traditional levels dressed up in new clothes — like Product/Audience & Portfolio Readiness. Others are genuinely new: their names and the technology behind them simply didn't exist twenty years ago. There are technological levels like "Data & AI Literacy" and politically inflected ones like "Ethics, Sustainability, Citizenship" — and more. My running log keeps growing.

I realize I've (once again) opened a new door — and that I want a full overview of all didactic levels. Not as a final product, but as an intermediate one: the foundation for my later working list. To get there I need to collect, analyze, and consolidate. I know this could turn into a bigger job, so I call this the "big" list, or the "global list." Eventually it will be the "GLOBAL LIST." And yes — this mammoth list will be completely useless for actual teaching. I'll have to cut it down and ask myself: Which didactic levels actually matter in my classroom? How many can I realistically handle at once? Four? Eight? Twelve? Where's the pain threshold — or will it turn into a pain zone?

I want to use these didactic levels to vary tasks or entire learning environments — easier or harder — and the image I have in my head is "adjustment screws." But right now there are 23 adjustment screws in front of me, and that's too many. Still, I keep fleshing out the image. Take the geography unit Our Earth: instead of accepting a task from the textbook as given, I deliberately turn one adjustment screw — one didactic level. Does it stay a pure map-reading task? Or do I change the form of representation, work with climate and population data, open up the context, or shift the social arrangement into a cooperative evaluation? Right now this is nothing more than an idea in my head. And a question: will differentiation in my classroom look different a few months from now?

04

A Top-10 List as a Holding Place

It's Wednesday afternoon. I start a new chat and upload the conversation from last night. I ask it to trim the 23-item list down to a top 10. I also set two selection criteria: "adaptability" and "future relevance."

I look at the list of ten and save it as a fleeting intermediate step. But doubt sets in quickly: the shortcut doesn't go far enough. It doesn't replace the 23-item collection — it only shows which levels survive when filtered by the criteria of adaptability and future relevance. And "10. Network/System Level" doesn't operate on the same plane as my classroom. There has to be more. And I don't want a list of ten based on an incomplete list of twenty-three.

So I keep going. I reopen the full list and collect every conceivable didactic level, round after round, letting them flow unfiltered into the «big list».

After four rounds I sort the levels into three working categories. First: classic levels from traditional didactics (Klafki, Heimann, or Schulz). Second: expanded levels from competency-based education and media pedagogy. Third: future-relevant didactic levels that could gain importance for me in an increasingly digitalized, AI-shaped learning world.

I'm back at yesterday's list of twenty-three. But this time I push on. More rounds bring more levels to the surface. It's like panning for gold. Sometimes you find something, sometimes nothing.

05

From 23 to 34 Levels

As a further addition, I upload a set of documents from my hard drive in the next round. I ask GPT to scan them for any didactic levels not yet on the list, and it suggests adding 11 more. Part of its reasoning is that some of them «cut across» classic categories. I don't fully understand what it means by that — but I add the category anyway.

From the raw data I build a deduplicated list and sort it again into «traditional» (widely used in schools today) and futuristic (not yet widespread in schools, but significant for the future). New running total: 34 levels.

The list is not a taxonomy and not a model; it's my personal working tool. From it I want to build a more manageable list of didactic levels and turn those into concrete levers for classroom use. I pull it up in Word and notice I have to zoom out to 60% just to see it all.

As a working list it's no longer usable — which was the plan.

FOREST

«This isn't a list anymore — it's a forest.»

06

Is This Still Classic Didactics?

What have I actually built? Is it a list of didactic levels generated by AI on the basis of probability, then selected and sorted by me? It's certainly experimental — and it might land controversially in academic circles. So I start thinking about which established didactic reference models I could compare it against. I pick two somewhat arbitrarily: the criteria from Hilbert Meyer's book What Makes Good Teaching? and the competency framework from the University of Teacher Education Lucerne (PH Luzern). I want to hold my list of didactic levels up against both and see what happens.

You:
Take Hilbert Meyer's 10 quality criteria for good teaching, take data on competency-based instruction, and take the elements of competency-based teaching from PH Luzern. Are any of these reflected in the didactic levels?

What I'm really asking is: does this still count as didactics in the traditional sense? What happens when I let GPT check the 34-item list I built myself?

It maps every single level in my list to one of the quality criteria and adds a brief assessment. Here is an excerpt from the comparison with Hilbert Meyer's "10 Characteristics of Good Teaching":

I get a confirmation that the didactic levels in my list of 34 are directly or indirectly compatible. At the same time I notice: this isn't really a test. It confirms a lot when you ask it in that direction. I'll need to check differently.

I ask about potential for improvement and get a list of things I could "sharpen." For example, that I should explicitly anchor "Intelligent Practice" as a didactic level. I disagree and leave it out on purpose. Not because practicing intelligently is unimportant, but because it's the wrong kind of concept for this list. Practice reminds me of a teaching phase in the sense of Aebli, and "intelligent" describes the quality of that phase. For my Global List, though, I'm looking for didactic levels I can actually change in tasks or learning environments. Practice cuts across several levels — action, differentiation, time, task format — and stays important for that reason. It just doesn't belong as its own level in this list.
It has gotten late — this September evening. I close the computer and the chat on "didactic levels" fades from memory.

07

Finding Order in the Forest

08

Clustering: The List Becomes a Forest

Weeks later — it's October now and I'm on fall break — I stumble across the chat in the GPT sidebar. I pick the project back up. How do I move forward with 34 didactic levels?
I go through the list. I notice that some levels might be related to each other: content, representation, context — that's all "subject matter," "content." Can I represent these 34 didactic levels in a simpler way? As "cards," for example? By grouping similar levels together? I try combining thematically similar levels into "cards" and manage to form seven such groups. From now on I'll call these groups "clusters."

You:
Take these 34 levels and sort similar ones together. Do not delete any levels or information.

ChatGPT:
Summary of the didactic levels grouped into seven clusters:
1. Content, Representation & Context (6 levels)
2. Learning Processes, Cognition & Metacognition (5 levels)
3. Didactic Action, Sequence & Support (5 levels)
4. Sociality & Culture (4 levels)
5. Media, Technology & Infrastructure (4 levels)
6. Creativity, Production & Public Audience (4 levels)
7. Transformation, Ethics & Future (6 levels)

Note:
This 7-cluster grouping is the first clustering attempt GPT produced from 34 levels.
Later, Cluster 7 is dissolved; from there, six clusters emerge with future-skills and digitality markers.

I check its proposal and see that it maps the "social level" in Cluster 4 — but I can't find a "digital level" anywhere. It has distributed the digital elements across several clusters. I look more closely and notice that some clusters don't quite hold together. I start deconstructing the model. Over many rounds I dissolve clusters and redistribute the didactic levels into others. For some levels I add tags: [DIG] for digitality references, [A/D] for levels usable both analog and digitally, and [FUT] for future- and future-skills references.

I keep following this approach and ask myself how the clusters might touch each other. How does it handle didactic levels that overlap with other didactic levels? These are the levels that sit "partly across" the others — and that's what interests me now. For the time being I'll call them "cross-cutting dimensions."

09

What Is Digitality?

And so I begin a sorting process across several turns — moving elements into other clusters, dissolving whole clusters, or forming new ones. I dissolve Cluster "5 Media & Technology" and Cluster "3 Action & Support," for example, and distribute them across the remaining clusters. The goal at this point is clusters that are coherent in content and function, without overlap, and clearly distinct from each other. Into Cluster "5 Media & Technology" I put everything related to media, technology, and digital. But the devil is in the details. "Digital" also shows up in Cluster "1 Content & Context" and in Cluster "6 Production & Public Audience." Maybe "digitality" isn't a self-contained didactic level at all, not even a cluster, but something else entirely? Maybe it's a dimension that cuts across all the clusters?

You:
Do we have a "digital level"?

It visualizes my conflict by drawing digitality either as an isolated building block or as a cross-running support beam holding up other blocks. I reach for a kitchen image.

Is digitality a spice on the pizza
or the flour in the dough?


It doesn't answer my prompt question directly. Instead it lays out three options: once as a cross-cutting dimension, once as its own level, and once as its own cluster bundling "technology, media, data, ethics." How do I decide?

This is one of those moments where I can't say "right or wrong," "left or right," "A or B" — because I simply don't know yet. I try to picture additional cross-cutting dimensions running side by side or stacked on top of each other, and I realize my layered image doesn't hold. Digitality doesn't just sit at the bottom or the top. It cuts across several levels: media and technology, data and AI literacy, adaptive systems, and ethical technology reflection. In the list it shows up through tags; in the model, though, it stays cross-cutting.
So I stay with the cross-cutting dimension — and I suspect there are more of them. I also give up my original plan of clusters with no overlap and clean boundaries.

10

One Step Back for a Better View

The process keeps moving. Turn by turn. Round by round. Question – answer. Proposal – pushback. This is not a linear, forward-only development between a person and an AI. And sometimes it goes a step backward:

You:
Restore the two dissolved clusters 5 and 3.

A person would get annoyed. GPT is perfectly calm. Only rarely does it write in its Thinking: «now adopting my suggestion after all…».
I catch myself wondering: «what does it mean by that?»

Today I'm stuck. I've lost my bearings — the thread is gone. I'm in the middle of the forest and can't tell which way is forward. And with every round, GPT keeps offering more suggestions and options, even though what I need right now is clarity and structure. Then comes the temporary surrender, the time-out, or simply….

I need to take a step back. I need an overview. What's out there? Where does the work actually stand? I think about building that overview myself — and immediately it occurs to me that AI can do it faster and more completely.

So I have GPT produce a current status summary of the work. I print it out and lay the three pages on the kitchen table. And when I see everything spread out in front of me, the fog lifts and the clarity comes back. I pick up a ballpoint pen and start scribbling notes on the pages. Those three sheets become my companions for a few days. At home, on the bus, on the train, at school — whenever a thought or an idea shows up, I jot a quick note on the sheet and that's that.

I make a brief attempt to add a second numbering layer to sort the clusters. After a few steps I can see it: the system is getting too complicated. I move it to the archive.

11

The Global List Is Done

12

GPT and Mistral Meet

Over the past few weeks I've been working on this project not only with GPT but also with MISTRAL — sometimes using the exact same prompts. Today I want to bring them together.

Same topic, same goal — and MISTRAL has produced its own full overview of didactic levels too. It shows the same question answered by a different model. And it carries a different structure. This isn't meant to become a model ranking test, and I'm not interested in which model is right.

The only thing I care about is: «What from these two worlds will actually help me in the classroom?» When two models organize similar questions differently, it shows you where the structure is still unresolved. That's exactly why I put the MISTRAL version next to the GPT version. MISTRAL brings a model with five clusters, three cross-cutting dimensions, and several category tags. For my own working version, I don't take everything over one-to-one — I clarify the logic instead: the levels stay as the working fields, the clusters organize those levels, the tags mark particular connections, and the cross-cutting dimensions run across all the levels.

Now I have two versions — the MISTRAL version and the GPT version — and both look equally good. I can't judge which one fits me better in the details. So I need an outside perspective:

You:
I want critical, no-holds-barred feedback on this version.

I get an overview of strengths and problem areas, and I compare the two versions. On top of that, GPT produces a brief comparative analysis of both models and offers a terse recommendation: "Clarify the function, reduce the load on Cluster 1, and operationalize the cross-cutting dimension." Done. Statements like that don't solve anything for me — but they do show me what I have to decide myself. And I remind myself what this list is for. It's not meant to be a theoretical framework. It's a pool of ideas for a later working list.

The direction is set
and my inner voice says:
"Move forward, not deeper."

In the short time I've been working with AI, I've learned that you can always go deeper. It's like entering a forest. Sometimes it's dense thicket, sometimes you get caught in the undergrowth — and sometimes you stumble onto a clearing, a spring, or a crossroads.

I lay the two threads side by side. Starting from its collection, GPT took 34 didactic levels and made a new structure visible through groupings, digitality markers, and future-skills markers. MISTRAL doesn't just add four new levels — it brings a different order: five clusters, three cross-cutting dimensions, and category tags. For my working version, I settle on six clusters with 38 didactic levels. Added to that: tags and four cross-cutting dimensions — Digitality, Ethics & Responsibility, Agility & Experimental Mindset, and Critical Reflection.

13

The Global List Is Done

I'm not looking for a finished truth — I'm looking for an intermediate goal. Six clusters, thirty-eight didactic levels, visible tags, and four cross-cutting dimensions: that's my full overview now. Digitality doesn't stand as its own cluster; it runs alongside everything as a cross-cutting dimension. This is my «GLOBAL LIST». It's Version 5.3.

This final version isn't a mathematically complete truth — it's a usable working draft. Big enough to map the forest, yet still ordered enough to cut a smaller classroom list from it. But for my classroom, it's definitely too large. Time for something manageable — a short, practical list. I'm calling it the «small list», or, slightly more technical, the «LOCAL LIST».

Which of the thirty-eight levels should I put on that small list?
Which ones will I actually use to differentiate my teaching going forward?
