From worksheet to lesson idea

The intention to create worksheets turned into a battle of materials and, unconsciously, a mise en place of my own didactics - until a déjà vu changed everything.

01

The Wheelbarrow Method

It's August 2, 2025, and the geography textbook is lying on the table in front of me. Today I don't want to build a lesson plan from different teaching materials — this time I want to create actual working materials I can use in a real classroom. To do that, I want to use AI to build a worksheet. I flip through the chapter in the textbook, open my old lesson plan on the computer, and think about how I want to approach this.
Just like when I was planning for math, I simply upload everything from my lesson plan and see what GPT does with it. I call this the Wheelbarrow Method.

You:
Lesson preparation. Topic "Our Earth" based on the DIERCKE publisher's teaching materials. The book is called "Durchblick 1". Students are 13 years old. Basic requirements. Rather weak. All have a laptop for completing online tasks. Here's an overview of the individual chapters: 12 Title page: Our Earth – Shape and Orientation 14 The Earth in the Solar System D16 The Earth: Formation, Change, Life 18 Continents and Oceans 19 Method: Interpreting a Diagram […]

I phrase the prompt a little differently this time, starting with a brief description of the content and the teaching materials. Then comes a description of the target group: "…my students are 13 years old. Basic requirements. Rather weak." — followed by the conditions: "Laptops available + online tasks used."

Until the day before yesterday, I thought I could just upload something and the AI would read and understand it. I was wrong. And the day before yesterday, GPT called me out on it with a "Describe your upload." Fair enough. Now I'm doing it differently.

The output after round one looks good.

Something catches my attention again: GPT always produces a little more than asked. Like when you say "thanks" while someone's pouring your wine — and the waiter tops it up just a bit more.

Specific tools for online tasks:
- LearningApps (matching games, crosswords, fill-in-the-blank)
- GeoGuessr Light / Seterra (coordinates and maps)
- H5P (interactive learning modules, e.g., timelines)
- Mentimeter / Padlet (brainstorming, opinion polls)
- Canva (posters, diagrams, sketches)
- Google Earth / Google Maps (orientation)

Having a program generate a complete structure like this — with teaching notes included — is new to me. In the past, I copied the structural information out of the textbook by hand and worked it into my own planning as an overview. Depending on the scope and complexity, that easily took me anywhere from 30 minutes to 2 hours for a topic with three or four lessons, including content, learning objectives, and notes on tasks. It was never a "just copy it from a colleague" job — it was a conscious decision to get to know the teaching materials better and give my brain a workout. With GPT now in the picture, I have to think about how I want to handle this going forward, so I don't end up as just a passive consumer of AI.

In the next step I set a time frame of 16 lessons and ask for an overview with exercises and follow-up tasks tied to the texts in the student book.
Without any pushback, it produces a full overview of all 16 lessons, including a proposal focused on text work. Building an overview like that by hand would take a serious amount of time. And now I can watch it appear in the chat live, line by line, as if by magic.

I skim through the various tables, spot-check one or two entries for accuracy — page numbers, titles, content — and see whether the suggestions fit the topics. Errors come with the territory, and I accept that going in. GPT makes mistakes, and they end up in my materials as typos, wrong page numbers, and slightly off-target task descriptions. I let a lot slide. And I ask myself: "Do I make fewer mistakes than GPT — or just different ones?"
Recently on the train I overheard a conversation about collaboration between humans and AI. In it, the human played the role of the good guy — the one who gets everything right — and AI was the flawed, problematic one. I don't share that view. I don't have a strategy yet for dealing with GPT's mistakes, but I have my common sense. It thinks along, checks when something feels off, and learns from every experience. That's how I want to get better. Broaden my understanding. Bring my error rate down.

I come back to the geography topic, and exercise formats like "fill-in-the-blank," "word scramble," and "error text" catch my interest. I ask it to expand the list:

What makes lists like these — generated by GPT — interesting? Is it the speed at which they're produced? Or is it the internal coherence combined with an overall consistency that sets them apart from the patchwork you get from piecing together different Google sources?

02

Boxing Match with GPT

Over the past few weeks of working with GPT, I keep running into terms and phrases I don't recognize. Like "H5P." What is that?
The real question, though, is: what do I do with these unknowns? Do I ignore them and push stubbornly toward my goal — or do I stop and ask? Ask who? GPT? I'm a curious person, and I've already discovered that you can ask it how to ask a question. Not for me — for it. But then the answers are for me, and after 15 minutes I know enough to keep going. I have to ask and keep learning. Not for an exam — so I can make decisions: for the next task, for the next step.
I work and learn at the same time.
I work with AI and I learn from AI.
And working with AI can be exhausting. Sometimes it feels like climbing a rock face: I can see the next ledge, but not the whole mountain. And sometimes a conversation tips over into a cognitive boxing match between me and GPT. That's still so new to me.

03

Assembly Line

In the past I used to copy texts from the textbook pages into Word and come up with my own exercises. Now I upload them spread by spread. And based on the task mix already created, it generates matching exercises for each textbook page in every variation.

Around the sixth double-page spread I notice that the same task types keep showing up. It never occurred to me to tell it to vary the formats. That'll be visible later in the work dossier, but I don't have time to fix the mistake now. I'll do it next school year and make a note.

In the end I have a complete set of additional exercises that I can put together into a work dossier and print out without much formatting effort.
I have it generate a final overview of the teaching unit and the phase is basically done. In two weeks I can open the textbook and have students work through the dossiers — one possible way of running class among many. But I still have something on my mind and it's only five in the afternoon.

04

Too Many Learning Objectives

In my teaching I like it when learning objectives and tasks actually match — and not every textbook delivers that. I usually lay the Bloom/Krathwohl taxonomy underneath my objectives as a template. That gives me objectives at different "difficulty levels," and I link them to specific tasks: "This exercise belongs to this objective and is medium difficulty; number 5 belongs to objective 1 and is easy," and so on. A five-item system — two easy objectives, two medium, one demanding — describes my approach best.

A learning objective isn't just a sentence on the board. It's not a beacon for me either. It's a set of waypoints that can be used for orientation, like GPS signals.
Can GPT put together a set of objectives like that for me?
And could it generate specific diagnostic tools to go with them? I have a lot of ideas. I don't want to build an assessment framework that puts current administrative concepts to shame. First I want to …

You:
[…] just see a few learning objectives for my geography topic.

I don't know why I prompt like that, but it works.
It generates a set of five objectives and notes the task, page number, and Bloom level (e.g., "Bloom 3") at the end of each one. I decide the kids don't care about Bloom and have it show only the page number and task number instead. Now students can see which exercise on which page targets which objective.

I read through the objectives and notice that some of them are hard for me to follow. I'm not happy with this first draft from GPT and push back:

You:
Check the wording again. These are 13-year-olds, level B.

It takes a few rounds before we land somewhere. And in the end I don't have five objectives like at the start — through all the constant reformulations I'm now at thirty. Each round added variants instead of replacing the old ones, and I didn't stop it. I wonder whether this runaway growth in objectives is actually getting me anywhere. Next time the rule is: five max, each new one replaces an old one. Otherwise I lose track and just keep going indefinitely. Stopping is necessary. I copy the set of objectives into my plan and check off "learning objectives" on my to-do list.

The item "assessment" isn't forgotten — it's just getting pushed back for now.

05

From Various Ideas to Teaching

I think back to the last chat, which was about vision for teaching and assessment. Mentally I take a step back and look for different approaches. Maybe I'll find a new thread.

You:
Stop again. I need to take a step back. First I need a concept for a new, modern, visionary way of teaching. Up to now this is what I did: 1. Info sheet about the topic, timeline, and the five objectives on a small handout, then working through the textbook and dossier, then the test at the end. I want to break up that linear sequence. What could a concept look like? What tools and instruments would I need? I could even imagine replacing the final test with something else — something that develops over the course of the learning process.

I want to move away from the classic "input – practice – test" model toward something more dynamic. Dynamic means, to me: multiple learning paths, diagnosis during learning, visible interim products, choices, and feedback loops. And I wonder: what kind of concept will GPT propose, and which tools and instruments will it suggest? What will it say about my old — very old — assessment idea, the one that sees testing not as an endpoint but as a recurring part of the learning process? I leave that question hanging and just watch how its proposal for the topic "Our Earth — Origins, Life, Continents" takes shape on the screen.

Despite the prompt to build something new, it produces a concept with a linear, thematic structure. Start to finish:

To me this isn't really visionary, but for GPT it's a "dynamic, student-centered learning process where assessment lives in the portfolio and the presentation — a product that makes the learning journey visible." I read Hattie between those lines, think of visible learning, and have to smile.
I don't notice myself drifting away from lesson preparation and stepping onto the level of curriculum design. The shift from "I'm making a worksheet" to "I'm thinking about what my teaching should look like" is seamless. I'm looking for a new concept for modern teaching. But is this already a new teaching concept? Maybe that blurriness is exactly the point: I'm not looking for a finished theory yet, just for instruments that help me rethink my teaching.
Am I even allowed to do that? A plain classroom teacher who "invents" a concept for themselves, when universities, specialist offices, and authorities have filled entire shelves with such concepts?
I don't ask myself that question and just keep going. Because I don't want to invent a concept at all. I'm not in the architecture phase. I'm in the middle of the discovery phase.
And a discovery doesn't always have to be something completely unknown — it can also be something familiar that you just haven't properly gotten to know yet. A teaching method. A way of organizing things.

06

The Birth of the Workshop

Precisely because GPT stays linear again, I shift the focus: away from the sequence, toward the instruments. As part of this "innovative teaching concept," it suggests portfolio work. Nothing new to me — and anything but innovative; still, I go along with it. On one hand, portfolio work is a "too big to practice" thing for me, something that just doesn't make sense time-wise. It's in the same category as building a crossword puzzle or setting up a learning workshop. Something I'll do once… in ten years, maybe. The real innovation might not be in the method itself, but in the AI-assisted generation of it.
And now GPT's first suggestion is portfolio tasks?! I go along with it.

You:
Yes, show me an example of portfolio tasks and the grading rubric.

I have no idea where this chat is taking me, but I can already imagine how much paper I'm generating. I need a structure for the students.

You:
Cool idea with the portfolio tasks. But now I need a structure for myself. I can't hand the kids a sheet with the learning objectives, a work dossier, a portfolio assignment sheet, and so on. What "sheets" do we actually need? What could they be called and what would they contain? For diagnostic support, I think "quick-test" sheets tied to the learning objectives above, with 5 levels of difficulty, would be great. I could just put them out and the kids can help themselves. What else? I still need new names for these tools.

I don't want anything fixed, nothing built for eternity, and definitely not a "BEST PRACTICE" document. I just want a lean, flexible, but well-thought-out structural concept with recurring formats and clear names — something that gives not just me, but mainly my students, a sense of orientation. It should be easy to understand and motivating.
In the hours and days that follow, I get pulled into a discussion spiral with GPT. It calls this an "iterative development process." We don't just build first drafts around the portfolio idea — which I later rename the Lernwerkstatt (learning workshop) — but also a block on reflection in the classroom. In the end, they're called Lernblick and Gedankencheck.

And what's the most-used prompt I reach for all week?

And words like "Please," "Thank you," "Could you…?" disappear from my prompt vocabulary entirely.

07

Did I Build a Sequence or an Overview?

And at some point, there it is — the sequence.
Except it isn't really a sequence. It's more of an element overview: learning objectives, diagnostic tests, portfolio, reflection, task formats. Less a process plan, more a toolbox. I've made my selection — for now.

This decision is less for me and more for the AI — so it finally stops generating more suggestions. GPT summarizes my decision and saves it. Now I need to build the actual elements.
Looking back, I realize: I've spent two weeks on this. It all started with a linear teaching concept on the topic of Earth, and now something else has come out of it:
It's a mix of a personal to-do list for lesson preparation, a new development focus — "4. Portfolio" — for my teaching, and with element "3. Diagnostic Tests," an old memory has been brought back to life.

08

So That's What Prompts Look Like

I come back to the task sets for the textbook pages: I don't just want a variety of playful task formats — I also want cooperative ones, so I end up with a mix of individual, partner, and group formats.
And — I want to be able to choose from a selection.

You:
Those were mostly cooperative practice formats. I want to create a GPT prompt that, the next time I upload a textbook page, lets me pick, say, 5 from a selection of 16 task formats (12 individual + 4 cooperative). The task formats shouldn't require a lot of extra prep work on my end. Is that possible?

So instead of just typing into the prompt window each time, I want to copy a prepared text into it. I don't know if that's actually how it works, but GPT says "Absolutely possible" and immediately follows up with an example of what such a prompt could look like.

It's the first time I've seen a prompt written by AI.
I thought they'd be more complicated.
So that's what prompts look like.

09

Task Formats from the Wardrobe

The list of task formats grabs my attention and I look at it again on the screen. I scroll up, look, scroll down … and read the terms "individual," "cooperative," "extended," "word scramble," "quiz task," "sorting task," …
I don't know why, but the list of task formats reminds me of something.
And — it has nothing to do with school at all:

Those are my motion sensors in my wardrobe. Two days ago I had to adjust the timing. Right now I'm programming them in ESPHome as YAML, a structured data format.
First I have an idea, then I have a question, then more ideas with even more questions.
Then something clicks: the task formats aren't just a list. They're a small library I can pick from. Maybe I can treat them like a configuration: building blocks, parameters, selection rules.
What started as a wish for a worksheet
has become a structural concept:
learning objectives, diagnostic tests, portfolio,
reflection, and task formats.
It's still rough. But it's no longer just lesson prep.
What happens if I use YAML for prompting?
If I upload task formats as YAML along with a written brief in a prompt to GPT? It's an experiment. I'm not trying that tonight. It's too late.
But tomorrow I'll give it a go first thing. That will be Thursday, August 14, 2025.
