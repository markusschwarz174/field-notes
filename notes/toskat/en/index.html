01

The Rigid High Jump Bar

Ever since I started teaching, I've often watched students who can't grasp what a text is saying or put it into their own words. It feels like two processes are running at the same time while they read. One I can see: eyes on the page, listening, nodding. The other I can't see. It happens inside their heads. That's where it's decided whether reading becomes understanding. Just because I can see and hear the reading process doesn't mean the understanding process is actually taking place.

During read-alouds or silent reading, it sometimes seems to me like the students and the text are living in two completely separate worlds. Why do I think that? Because of the answers I get to questions like: "What did you just read?" or: "Why does that matter?" I want reading to actively trigger something in students — and to leave a lasting mark.

What's actually in my hands? Should I find a different text from a different textbook? Should I rewrite the existing one to make it more engaging, or simplify it? I usually don't have time for that. Or should I take the easy way out: I've provided the student textbook, and that's what we use. After all, nobody helps you later in life when you don't understand a text.

But that doesn't make the problem go away. The text stays the same — regardless of who's sitting in front of it and what obstacles come up while reading. So the question isn't just whether a student understands the text. The question is also what conditions I could change to make understanding possible in the first place. I don't see my job as placing students in front of an impossible barrier and then grading whether they clear it or not. My job is to create the conditions under which students both want to jump higher and are actually able to.

That doesn't work with a bar set at the same height for everyone. I need an adjustable bar — most likely one that can be individually adapted to each student's needs. In concrete terms: I don't want to simply dumb down the same text for every student. I want to create individual versions. And those versions should match different reading goals. A student shouldn't just get a "simplified" text — they should get a text they can actually work with and use to show what they've taken in.

02

I'm Daydreaming

But what does an adjustable high jump bar for texts even look like?
It's Monday, January 5, 2026, and I'm still on break. Once again I'm wrestling with GPT, trying to figure out how to extract content from student textbook pages and get it ready for class. I'm picking up a project I started back in August. But on this particular day the whole thing is so frustrating that I stare at the page and think: "Do something other than experimenting. Something that actually makes sense."

While I'm still looking at the history textbook page, a conversation with Reed — our school's special education teacher — comes to mind. We'd talked about students' reading skills and how hard it is for some of them to make sense of texts and tasks.

"You need to write a student textbook text for students, not for historians," I suddenly think, grinning at the page like I'm facing a new opponent in a duel. Of course a student textbook text has to be factually correct and pedagogically sound. But for a moment I push the purely subject-didactics lens aside and focus on the learner's perspective: what does a text actually need to do in order to reach students at all? I want a student textbook text they can understand.

Not because it's simpler, but because it opens a door for them.

Because it might interest them and give them something to connect it to. Maybe something from their own lives? Maybe the text sparks something because it leads to more than just a completed assignment: a question, a thought, a conversation with someone else. And that exchange with other people might lead to deeper understanding, to sharing, to passing something on.
Really, all I want is a text close enough to students that they actually want to start reading. That would be something.
I open my eyes, look at the textbook page, and ask myself:
"And how exactly?"

03

Which Dials Make Texts Easier or Harder?

The question won't let me go. If texts are going to become adjustable high jump bars, I first need to figure out what can actually be adjusted. That means diving into the world of language didactics, EdTech, and AI tools. I'm looking for the dials I can turn to make a text easier or harder. Which properties of a text can I, as a teacher, actually change? Sentence length? Word choice? Structure? Examples? Technical terms?

I know enough to ask first questions, but not yet enough to build a tool from them. I pause for a moment and think: "Honestly, you never know enough. And that's been true since long before July." That doesn't mean I know nothing — it just means there's a voice that keeps saying: "There's more to discover here."

Since July I've been calling this kind of research my "software update": I ask GPT or Claude a question, read the answer, and find the next question inside it. That's how I build up just enough knowledge, step by step, to search more precisely. And that's exactly how I start here:

You: You are a language didactics specialist and an expert in differentiating texts by level for different student groups. Context: I have learned that […] Explain which parameters a teacher can effectively change and which cannot be changed. Are there scales for these parameters — dials one can turn, among other things?

That's the first prompt of a longer session. In the following rounds I'm not yet working on any actual text — I'm just trying to get my bearings. GPT doesn't give me a ready-made solution, but it gives me a structure: terms, models, new questions. And I end up learning a lot about something I encounter every day but had never really thought about in these terms.

How do I make text difficulty measurable? I collect terms like groceries in a basket: sentence length, word length, syllable count, syntactic complexity, academic language, German-as-a-second-language pedagogy, language-sensitive content instruction. I don't yet know which of these will actually be useful for my tool. For a moment I find myself wondering: «What would Björnsson's research have looked like if he'd already had GPT or Claude available?»

Looking across the different approaches, one thing becomes clear quickly: text difficulty can't be captured with a single formula. Some properties are easy to count. Sentence length, word length, syllable count, number of subordinate clauses — those can be measured. Other factors resist a simple scale: prior knowledge, interest, cultural familiarity, or whether an example resonates with teenagers at all. And even among the measurable properties, I find different classification systems. Subordinate clause depth is represented on a scale in some models, while others work with percentages or formulas. I can't find a single shared logic that would let me control all of a text's properties in one place.

That gap is exactly what starts to interest me. I have Claude and GPT put together a summary on «BLIND SPOTS IN RESEARCH — What the research CANNOT measure well.» These chat sessions with AI feel like a postcard from an unknown country. I just follow my curiosity. After a while I realize this knowledge matters. I'm dealing with something I can't quite get my hands around yet.

I keep collecting adjustment parameters. The goal isn't a complete encyclopedia — just a usable working list. From all the suggestions I put together a first table with 16 parameters. This isn't the final list yet. It's a snapshot: I want to get all the dials written down in one place.

Later that same day I look at the parameter table and start imagining how I could move the sliders.

That's how I could make a text easier or harder. I picture a set of rotary dials that I move in the same direction at the same time. Depending on where they sit, a new text level emerges.

04

From Sweet Spot to a Manageable List of 7

I'm relieved that the working list has «only» 16 parameters for differentiating texts and not even more. But I quickly notice that these parameters aren't mathematical dials. Some things AI handles well — word count, sentence length, certain sentence patterns. It gets harder where meaning, motivation, or cultural fit come into play. AI can shorten a subordinate clause or swap out a difficult word. But whether a simplified text is still precise enough, and whether it actually connects with teenagers — that it can't decide on its own. Which makes things complicated: how do I have AI change something when the effect only shows up in the classroom?

And that leads me to the question: «What can AI actually do?» In my first months with GPT I assumed AI was limitless. Now I'd have to ask more carefully: «What can it do — and what can't it?» And even that answer only holds for the moment, because tomorrow it might already be different.

My impatience sometimes makes me skip a development step. So I start experimenting with a few parameters on some early texts. Working with the «shorten» parameter, I notice there are conflicts between parameters. Some of them just aren't compatible with each other. If I shorten sentences, information density goes up. If I reduce technical terms, precision suffers. Adapting texts isn't a simple dial-turning exercise — it's a trade-off between readability and accuracy.

I don't have a solution for this dilemma yet, but there's something kind of interesting about it. It becomes clear to me that my original plan — using all parameters at the same time — amounts to a brute-force attempt. That won't work. I can't upload a spreadsheet with 16 parameters and expect GPT or Claude to give me something sensible in return. I'm going to have to make a selection.

A single text I probably could have edited by hand and been done with it long ago. But I don't just want to change one text — I want to be able to change and compare many texts going forward. For a while now I've been working with GPT and Claude in parallel. It's like playing tennis with three people. I keep going and ask Claude:

You: How many "parameters" can AI reasonably handle when generating a text?

I watch the expanded lines in the "Thinking" section and I genuinely laugh when I read this:

Claude: "This is a meta-question about my own capabilities and limitations."

An output that sounds like metacognition. I'm genuinely surprised — and fascinated. Even though it looks like self-evaluation, it doesn't actually show me what's happening inside the model. But I enjoy watching it, and I catch myself wondering: "Who taught it to do that?"

As expected, the answer isn't simply: "16 parameters are fine." The combinations are what matter. Claude walks me through in detail which parameters cause no problems and which ones can conflict with each other. The short answer is:

CLAUDE: Sweet spot: 5–7 parameters remain reliably controllable at the same time. Still manageable: 8–12 parameters, but with increasing trade-offs. Breakdown zone: From 13 parameters onward, conflicts and inconsistencies rise sharply.

I'm not treating these numbers as scientific findings — just as a rough orientation from the AI dialogue. A few parameters stay controllable; too many create conflicts. In this conversation, both GPT and Claude flag that conflicts can start to increase around eight simultaneously controlled parameters. That's useful input for a priority list I'll need later — a list that defines which parameters win when they contradict each other.

So I start fresh — empty list, new development phase. I test each parameter with questions similar to the ones I used for the didactic levels: Can an AI change it deliberately? Can I define clear values or descriptions for A, B, and C? And does it overlap too much with another parameter? Then I check the parameters for conflicts, conditions, and dependencies, shuffle them around on the list, and try to answer one question: "What should an AI do when it can't decide what matters more?"

After several rounds, seven features remain — drawn from five feature groups.

I think of them like the faders on a stereo system: each one adjustable on its own, but only together do they determine how difficult a text becomes.

I'm a little impatient. I want to try the list right away and adapt a text. So I upload a non-fiction text along with the 7-parameter list to GPT and say:

You: Create a new version of the text at Level B. Use the parameter values from the list.

I want to check the generated text. GPT suggests a LIX of 40 to 45 for level B. I can't find a solid source or any list that says: "7th grade, level B equals exactly LIX 40 to 45." Still, the number isn't pulled out of thin air. So I treat this range not as a scientific target value, but only as a rough orientation from my AI dialogue. And I learn that I can't simply transfer reference values from other languages into German. I also swap the fixed word count for a more flexible approach using a reference value and relative terms: level B equals 100 percent. Level C covers maybe 50 to 70 percent of that, level A 130 to 150 percent.

For each of the seven features, I have values generated for levels A, B, and C. That turns the list of seven into a first controllable set — one that combines percentages, absolute values, and verbal descriptions.

Word length I let other parameters handle indirectly. I add LIX as a validation metric. Meaning: after the AI has scaled the text, the workflow checks whether the text lands roughly in the target LIX range. And it works.

Three days later, on January 8th, I could once again say: "Done. Project complete." But I'm not satisfied yet. It's more complicated than I thought. I can now differentiate across three levels. But does this work universally, across the board? Do I actually have a tool that works in my classroom in general? Is this list really right?

05

Start Building

Over the past few months, I've seen how many differentiation options exist in education. And then there are differentiated differentiation options, and so on. There are probably infinitely many of them. "Infinite is a lot," I tell myself — and start with two.

You: […] work iteratively with 5 "hard" and 2 "nice to have" parameters from the list of seven.

For this first attempt, I treat sentence length, text volume, technical terms, sentence structure, and level of abstraction as hard parameters. Verb style and connectors come in as optional controls for fine-tuning. This isn't a universal order yet. It's my first attempt at weighting the seven dials. But right away, the next question surfaces from the cross-check phase: what happens when two features create a conflict that iteration can't resolve? For example, simplicity versus subject accuracy. At the tertiary level, subject accuracy can't be precise enough; simplicity barely matters there. At level B, I can't take that road. There, accessibility and comprehensibility matter more than maximum subject precision. A text nobody understands may be factually correct, but it's useless in the classroom. That's exactly where simplicity comes in. If it comes to it, I'm willing to pull back on academic precision in favor of comprehensibility — without touching factual correctness. I'll take criticism from subject experts, as long as the text stays factually accurate and the simplification is didactically justified. That's my call. That's my lever.

Ranking the features by priority also creates a conflict hierarchy. It tells the AI what matters more when there's a clash. From this, a three-phase workflow takes shape: first come the five hard parameters. Their job is to produce a working text — not polished yet. Then the two optional parameters for fine-tuning. Finally, the checks and readjustments.

Then I get the idea to vary the flexible part by subject context. A science text might need different settings than a text from German class or geography and history. My rigid 5+2 setup becomes a 4+3 mental model. That makes everything more complicated again. I need new roles and have to rework the conflict matrix. I start testing the model for consistency and pushing it further.

You: Analyze my answers and check for consistency, redundancies, and gaps, so that a specific "text generation" section can then be created for the YAML.

The more precisely I define the dials, the more clearly I see their limit: no metric can tell me whether a simplified text actually works in the classroom. For that, I need someone who knows the students.

I think of Reed, the school's special education teacher. Maybe he sees something from his angle that my metrics don't. We talk through text differentiation and I listen to his take. How do the texts actually land? Is easy really easy? And is a special-ed-adapted text genuinely more readable for a student with dyslexia than the original textbook version?

I've known from the start that differentiating texts isn't an exact science. That's why I need Reed's feedback. His experience isn't captured in a metric — and it's not something I can just look up in the literature.

After a few more rounds, I have a draft. Depending on the text type and source, I can now weight and adjust the same seven features differently — without having to invent a new list every time. I now think of my little list of seven as a template. To start, I build three simple variants:

Nonfiction text: simplify sentences, make the structure clearer, and carefully replace difficult words. STEM: keep technical terms but explain them; make processes and cause-and-effect relationships clearer. Literary text: simplify the language only with care, and preserve mood and meaning as much as possible.

The seven features of my 7-item list stay in place as a didactic thinking model. But their roles aren't fixed universally. In the first 5+2 configuration, I treat five as "hard" and two as optional fine-tuning. In the later 4+3 model, four form the core and three are prioritized depending on context. The YAML doesn't implement the seven parameters as a closed list: it combines the core and context templates and uses the 4+3 model as a fallback.

The next day, January 9th, I open a chat and just start writing…
You: I'm interested in the theoretical possibilities of differentiating texts with the help of AI, and the background behind it. I'm interested in empirical and scientific foundations, and how AI works. I want to iteratively develop a model and create a YAML with parameters that I can use to instruct AI to scale texts […]

The prompt keeps going and ends up being over 300 words long. I write down everything I'm carrying in my head — the workflow, the global feature list, possible biases, and first experimental parameters. Out of that, a YAML configuration takes shape, and with it a possible answer to the key question: How do I get all these dials to work together reliably?

06

I'm Not Building a Worksheet Anymore

Up to this point, I've mostly been working on the dials: Which features of a text can I change? Which ones matter more than others? Now I realize: good dials alone aren't enough. I need an architecture that makes sure they get applied reliably. The basic idea seems simple at first: I upload everything and at the end I get my adapted reading text.

TOSKAT is meant to work in two ways. On one hand, as a component inside a larger system — for example, a worksheet generator. On the other hand, as a standalone tool for scaling texts. For that, I need an interaction logic.

When does the AI decide? When do I decide? When does the system check the text? And when do I get a report showing me what was changed? But the most important thing for me is something else: after uploading, I don't want to have to think through how everything works every single time. Within two minutes, it should be clear what I need to enter and what happens next. The one-shot form is my first attempt to make that as simple as possible. While saving in Visual Studio Code, I notice something else:

I'm no longer using AI to create a worksheet.

I'm building a tool with AI — one I understand myself, can test, and can rebuild. I haven't fully realized it yet, but I notice: what I'm doing doesn't have much to do with using anymore. It has more to do with developing. And that's exactly how it feels.

The YAML configuration from January 9, 2026 runs to about 2,650 words and 831 lines. It's the work of several days. In the end, I didn't just build TOSKAT as a component of a future framework. Without realizing it, I also wrote an architectural blueprint for additional prompt-YAML models. TOSKAT is a LEGO brick. With the blueprint, I can build more LEGO bricks. The system has two layers: the YAML describes what gets controlled — parameters, values, and rules. The master prompt describes how the AI interprets and executes that configuration. I can develop both layers separately, but in sync. And the most important thing: I know what is written, where, and why — because I understand how my code works. And TOSKAT? That stands for "TOol for Scaling TeXTs."

Workshop thought

I didn't write this alone. AI didn't write it alone either.

It's a co-construction — human intent, AI suggestions, and my decisions.

07

Louis XIV: Does My Tool Actually Work?

Three days later, on Monday, January 12, I want to try out my tool — once in Claude and once in GPT. I upload the YAML, the master prompt, and a textbook page on absolutism. Then I watch what happens. Claude checks the files and shows me the one-shot form.

And I notice something new: there are answer fields you can click. I've never seen that in Claude before. I think it's pretty cool. I fill out the form like an order card at a pizza delivery place. Then I wait. Now I realize what kind of task I've actually given Claude. It takes a long time, and Claude writes: "One moment …" That rarely means anything good. But in the end, it works.

After each adjustment, Claude checks the new text. Several times the results fall outside the target values and the workflow starts over. I picture AI fighting an internal battle between LIX, WSTF, and Flesch — three readability indices I built in specifically for this. Claude wins. In the end I have three different texts on the topic of absolutism. Here is level B:

At first glance, the reading text looks fine. Now I run the same process with GPT. It also recognizes the files, queries the parameters, and delivers output plus a mini report. But the path it takes looks different from Claude's.

GPT scans the textbook page, breaks it into blocks, lets me choose the source text, and displays its task one more time before generating anything. The workflow runs, and at the end, output and mini report appear on the screen. Are the three texts actually different?

Yes. The metrics are spread apart and the texts feel differently difficult. In this first test, TOSKAT works on a technical level: the workflow runs through, and the generated texts differ in their metrics. But a LIX score only shows that something changed. It doesn't tell me whether a student actually understands the text better. What matters is whether something sticks — whether someone asks a question, keeps reading, or can explain the content in their own words. I don't have that feedback yet, and without it, TOSKAT is just a toy on my screen.

That same afternoon I have a Geography/History lesson. I don't want to run a big test like I did in December with math. So after a short introduction and giving the assignment, I ask almost as an aside: "Does anyone want to work with a GPT sheet?"

Then I explain briefly that I've built a tool that can make texts easier or harder. For the double-page spread on "Absolutism in France," I used Claude to create three reading texts at different difficulty levels. And I want to find out whether they're actually easier or harder. And whether anyone is interested in checking that. The answer is neither "yes" nor "no" — it's: "Really?"

I don't know why some students pick up one of the three versions I've put out. It's about half the class. That's exactly why the rest of the lesson gets interesting. I can focus on a small group while the other half works independently on the learning dossier. As I walk around and ask questions, I talk with the students about what feels hard or easy for them. It's words, passages, and content from the absolutism text. They compare the texts, mark sections, and explain to each other why something is easy or not. I've never seen students work with a text this intensely. I didn't see this coming.

I have to admit, once again, that I really need to improve how I set up these tests. This first attempt shows me reactions and new questions, but not much in the way of actual learning gains. I completely underestimated the potential of this lesson, and at the end I tell myself: "You have to do this again."

The following evening, Tuesday, January 13, 2026, I work on the YAML configuration one more time and save TOSKAT as version 2.5 on my laptop.

In March I want to use TOSKAT again for the new geography unit on Europe. Next time I want to look more carefully. I need to think about what I actually want to pay attention to and how I record that feedback. Then I don't just want to check whether the tool works — I want to know whether it genuinely helps the students.
